# Lecture 1: Introduction to AI and Historical Context

## What is Artificial Intelligence?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to systems or machines that mimic human intelligence to perform tasks and can iteratively improve themselves based on the information they collect. AI manifests in a number of forms:

1. **Narrow or Weak AI**: Designed to perform a narrow task (e.g., facial recognition, internet searches, driving a car)
2. **General or Strong AI**: Systems with generalized human cognitive abilities that can find solutions to unfamiliar tasks

Currently, all existing AI systems are examples of narrow AI. General AI remains a theoretical concept and a long-term research goal.

## Historical Evolution of AI

### Early Foundations (1940s-1950s)

- **1943**: Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts propose the first mathematical model of a neural network
- **1950**: Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," introducing the Turing Test
- **1951**: Marvin Minsky and Dean Edmonds build SNARC, the first neural network computer
- **1956**: The Dartmouth Conference officially coins the term "Artificial Intelligence"

### The First AI Winter (1974-1980)

After initial enthusiasm and substantial government funding, AI research faced criticism and significant funding reductions. This period is known as the "AI Winter," characterized by:

- Limitations of existing computing technology
- Combinatorial explosion problems
- Failure to meet ambitious expectations
- Critique by philosophers about the fundamental possibility of machine intelligence

### Expert Systems Era (1980s)

Expert systems—programs designed to replicate the decision-making ability of human experts—gained prominence:

- Rule-based systems that captured domain-specific knowledge
- Commercial success in specific industries
- Limitations in scalability and adaptability

### The Rise of Machine Learning (1990s-2000s)

The focus shifted from hard-coded rules to systems that could learn from data:

- Development of algorithms that improve through experience
- Bayesian methods and statistical approaches
- Support Vector Machines and early neural network revivals

### Deep Learning Revolution (2010-Present)

Breakthroughs in neural networks, particularly deep learning, have transformed AI capabilities:

- **2012**: AlexNet demonstrates the power of deep learning in image recognition
- **2016**: AlphaGo defeats world champion Go player
- **2018-2020**: Transformer architectures revolutionize NLP
- **2022-2023**: Large language models (ChatGPT, etc.) demonstrate remarkable capabilities

## AI in Modern Society

Today, AI technologies are integrated into numerous aspects of daily life:

- **Personal assistants**: Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant
- **Recommendation systems**: Netflix, Amazon, Spotify
- **Autonomous vehicles**: Tesla, Waymo
- **Healthcare**: Diagnostic tools, drug discovery
- **Financial services**: Fraud detection, algorithmic trading

## Ethical Considerations

As AI becomes more pervasive, several ethical concerns have emerged:

1. **Bias and fairness**: AI systems can perpetuate or amplify existing biases
2. **Privacy concerns**: Data collection necessary for AI raises privacy issues
3. **Job displacement**: Automation's impact on employment
4. **Accountability**: Who is responsible when AI makes mistakes?
5. **Existential risk**: Long-term concerns about advanced AI systems
